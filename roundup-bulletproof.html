Good morning and happy May Day folks. May Day in America is one of those holidays that has so many origins that most don't know what to celebrate. There's the pseudo-Roman version, including maypoles (which I definitely remember doing in elementary school), that was an effort by wealthy “social reformers” of the 1800s to instill "Anglo-Saxon" values on the country's growing migrant class. — Smithsonian

Then there's the International Worker's Day that is still celebrated in much of the world — despite the fact that it started with a worker's strike in 1886 Chicago for an eight-hour work day — Time.

Most recently — and controversially — May 1 was christened “Loyalty Day” by President Eisenhower as a counter measure to labor activists, and every president since has continued the tradition — yes, even the Democrats. — WaPo

But the best thing about May Day being on May 1 is that it's almost always a nice day, so choose your celebration (or none at all) and enjoy it.

OK, history lesson over, let's get to the news.

Two sides of Colorado's opioid epidemic

91

That's how many people Vernon Lewis, a former homeless addict, has personally saved from opioid overdose by administering naloxone injections — which are currently being administered nearly three times daily in Colorado. As part of his work with the Harm Reduction Action Center, Lewis is known as the “Uber of Narcan” or the “naloxone ninja,” willing to borrow a bicycle in the middle of the night to reach someone who has overdosed (Jennifer Brown has much more on Lewis and the drug's use in Colorado).

“One of the most devastating things to watch is somebody that you love slowly kill themselves.”

Rep. Brittany Pettersen, speaking about her mother, Stacy

Off the streets and in the Capitol, a different facet of the opioid crisis is taking shape thanks to Lakewood Democrat Brittany Pettersen, whose mother Stacy is a prescription drug addict and as recently as last month had overdosed. John Frank looks at how her mother's experience has driven Pettersen to push the state to create more robust substance abuse programs.

Daniel Brenner, Special to The Denver Post

Representative Brittany Pettersen and her mother Stacy pose for a picture at their home in Lakewood.

Firestone explosion launches new debate over old pipelines

Eric Lubbers

Image Test for Outlook

In the days since the deadly explosion at a home in Firestone, wells operated by Anadarko and now Great Western have been shuttered while investigations are completed. One point of contention: pressure tests to determine leaks that may not have been performed when the well closest to the home was restarted.

A different angle, and a stickier one, is also being investigated. State regulators adopted new rules establishing big buffers between new drilling and homes, but left it up to cities like Firestone (current rule: 150 feet) to determine how close new homes could be to old wells. Chris Osher and Bruce Finley's story is a must read for anyone living near — or potentially buying near — some of the state's thousands of wells.

Old Sports Authority HQ climbing out of vacancy

Now this is an idea I can get behind: Earth Treks, already the world's largest indoor climbing gym company, will be taking over the sprawling former Sports Authority headquarters in Englewood as their own — including building the world's largest climbing gym. And that's not all, writes Jason Blevins: The company will be eyeing the abundant carcasses of Sports Authority stores around the country to build a network of their branded climbing gyms.
